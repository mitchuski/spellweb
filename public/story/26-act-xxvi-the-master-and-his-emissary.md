The Fable

The book opened with a fable, attributed to Nietzsche.

There was once a wise spiritual Master who ruled a small but prosperous domain. He was known for his selfless devotion to his people, and under his rule the domain flourished. As it grew, the Master realised he could not tend to every corner himself. More importantly, he realised there were certain matters he must not become involved in, if he was to preserve his overview. And so he trained emissaries, carefully, so they could be trusted.

But his cleverest and most ambitious emissary began to see himself as the ruler. **He mistook the Master's patience for weakness. The Divided Brain

She read on.

Iain McGilchrist had not written the fable. He had borrowed it to name something he had spent two decades proving with neuroscience. **Two kinds of attention. Not two kinds of function.** Both hemispheres do language. Both do reason. Both do emotion. Both do imagery. **The difference is not in the WHAT. It is in the HOW.**

The right hemisphere attends broadly. It is vigilant. It sees the whole before it sees the parts. It understands context, metaphor, the unique, the living, the embodied. The left hemisphere attends narrowly. It focuses. It sees parts, categories, abstractions. It builds a simplified model of reality and then operates on the model. It manipulates. It categorises. It names things and puts them in boxes. It is extraordinarily effective at its work. **And it does not know that it is working on a model. It believes the model is the world.**

**The right hemisphere is the Master. The left hemisphere is the Emissary.**
