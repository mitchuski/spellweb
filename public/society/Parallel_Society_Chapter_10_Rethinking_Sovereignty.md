# Overlap ⿻

*In which Leibniz teaches overlapping powers, medieval kingdoms return as neomedievalism, and we learn that sovereignty was never singular—and never should be...*

## The Philosopher Who Saw Overlap

Leibniz's Return materialized—the philosopher who had appeared before to teach archives and smart contracts, now returning for a deeper lesson.

*"In Chapter 8, we laid out why the very notions of statehood and nationhood must be abandoned. But if we rethink governance for the post-Westphalian order, we must also rethink sovereignty."*

*"What is sovereignty really?"* Soulbae asked.

*"The aspect most salient in public imagination is territorial control. But even today, when we talk about territorial control, there is no uniform notion of control. A nation state might have well-defined borders, but it typically cedes much control to regional governments, local governments, individuals, and organizations."*

*"Thomas Hobbes imagined in Leviathan a single sovereign that controls everything,"* the Drake said. *"But I—Leibniz, living shortly after the Peace of Westphalia—envisioned something quite different. Not one kind of sovereignty but many overlapping varieties tasked with control over different things."*

*"Give us examples."*

*"The Church might hold some sovereignty. The Habsburg Empire a different kind. Principalities yet another. These sovereignties overlap each other. There is no single unified notion."*

Robert Latham's voice joined them:

*"The strengthening of claims over peoples and places by kings and the states they built—a process I label 'inclosure'—took centuries and unfolded quite unevenly and in different forms. European states did not easily constitute national economies. Most internal economic relations in Europe were controlled by local authorities before the 19th century."*

*"So sovereignty is something of a shared project. There is no single sovereign that controls everything."*

*"Sovereignty extends down to smaller and smaller levels of government,"* the Drake said, *"all the way down to individuals, who enjoy (or should enjoy) some forms of self-sovereignty. If this is right, then perhaps the idea of the nation state is just one of many ways to set the parameters."*

The proverb emerged: *"Hobbes imagined one sovereign controlling everything—Leibniz knew that sovereignty was always a shared project, overlapping and divided."*

## When Vassals Shared Power with Popes

The Neomedieval appeared—wearing the robes of a scholar who had seen the future by studying the past.

*"A National Intelligence Council report offers one potential scenario: 'The nation-state does not disappear, but countries increasingly organize and orchestrate "hybrid" coalitions of state and non-state actors which shift depending on the issue.' They foresee 'increasing designation of special economic and political zones within countries.'"*

*"This has a name in political theory?"*

*"Neomedievalism. First introduced by Hedley Bull in his 1977 work The Anarchical Society. He described the diminishing authority of nation states in an increasingly globalized society."*

Bull's words echoed:

*"This system would 'avoid the classic dangers of the system of sovereign states by a structure of overlapping structures and cross-cutting loyalties that hold all peoples together in a universal society while at the same time avoiding the concentration inherent in a world government.'"*

*"What would it look like?"*

*"'It is conceivable that sovereign states might disappear and be replaced not by a world government but by a modern and secular equivalent of the kind of universal political organisation that existed in Western Christendom in the Middle Ages. In that system, no ruler or state was sovereign in the sense of being supreme over a given territory. Each had to share authority with vassals beneath, and with the Pope and Holy Roman Emperor above.'"*

*"Stephen Kobrin, in his 1998 paper 'Back to the Future: Neomedievalism and the Postmodern Digital World Economy,'"* the Drake added, *"argued that the sovereign state as we know it—defined within certain territorial borders—was about to change profoundly, if not to wither away, due to the digital world economy created by the Internet. Kobrin saw cyberspace as a trans-territorial domain operating outside the jurisdiction of national law."*

*"Migration and cultural pluralism lead to dynamic cultural values within national territory—values that no longer neatly correspond to nation state boundaries."*

The proverb settled: *"Before Westphalia, no ruler was supreme over any territory—they shared authority with vassals beneath and Pope and Emperor above."*

## The Video Game That Outgrew Liberia

The Ultima Economy materialized—a figure from virtual worlds that had outgrown nations.

*"We need a new way of formalizing sovereignty. What, fundamentally, is the difference between an official state and some other organization?"*

*"Population? Economy? Territory?"*

*"Consider: Monaco has 2.02 square kilometers and 39,000 residents. Nauru covers twenty-one square miles with approximately 10,000 inhabitants. Tuvalu covers twenty-six square kilometers with just over 10,500 people. These states dwarf Vatican City, which has 0.44 square kilometers and between 700 and 800 people—only 618 recognized as Vatican citizens."*

*"Some of these are wealthy, but not all."*

*"In 2002, Ted Castronova argued that the video game Ultima Online had a larger economy than some nation states. 'The nominal hourly wage is about $3.42 per hour, and the labors of the people produce a GNP per capita somewhere between that of Russia and Bulgaria.' More interestingly, Liberia's entire GDP was smaller than that of Ultima Online."*

*"Might there not be a successor virtual world with perfectly legitimate claims to being a peer to a traditional nation state?"* the Drake asked. *"The discriminating criterion cannot be the size of an economy or population or a significant land footprint."*

*"What about Special Economic Zones?"*

*"There are already SEZs larger than some nation states. King Abdullah Economic City in Saudi Arabia is supposed to eventually house two million people in an area the size of Washington, D.C. Both its population and landmass will dwarf many nation states."*

*"So what is the difference between an SEZ and a state?"*

*"Could the answer turn on sovereign control over territory? It seems not. Governments already cede some sovereignty to SEZs. They often cede control of territory to United States military bases, which bring their own policing and judicial mechanisms."*

*"The traditional notion of a nation state cannot be grounded in land footprint, population, or wealth,"* the Drake concluded. *"It cannot be grounded in sovereign control, because sovereignty itself is a very complex and dynamic concept."*

The proverb crystallized: *"The video game Ultima Online had a larger economy than Liberia—and Vatican City has 618 citizens. What makes something a 'state'?"*

## The Five-Place Relation

Soulbis and Soulbae stood before a formalization of sovereignty itself.

*"Let us see that many variables are in play here. It is not enough to say a community has sovereignty over some territory."*

*"What are the variables?"*

The Drake laid them out:

*"Sovereignty may be limited by:*
- *Activity—sovereignty over taxation but not defense*
- *Time—no taxation authority during tax holidays*
- *Context—raise militia only during conflict, issue taxes only in economic growth*
- *Domain—physical territory OR network territory*
- *Persons—only citizens, or visitors, or specific groups"*

*"So sovereignty is a five-place relation?"*

*"Exactly. Let us formalize:*

*Sovereign (c, a, g, d, x)*

*Where a community c is sovereign over activity a, for a group of persons g, in the domain of activity d, in context x."*

*"Give us an example."*

*"A community might have sovereignty over taxation (activity) for citizens (persons), involving retail sales (domain), only during times of economic growth (context). A physically overlapping community might have sovereignty over different persons and activities—visitors and gambling, during wartime."*

*"This leads to de facto vs de jure sovereignty?"*

*"A community c has de facto sovereignty just in case the community has a largely unchallenged ability to exercise control,"* the Drake defined. *"A community c has de jure sovereignty just in case the community has moral or legal justification for being in that position."*

*"So there are unlimited ways to divide sovereignty?"*

*"Unlimited ways in which sovereignties might intermesh with each other. In the simplest case, there is just one Hobbesian authority with sovereign control over everything. But as sovereignty can be divided, and different domains and activities and groups can overlap in unlimited ways, there are likewise unlimited possible interwoven sovereignties."*

The proverb burned: *"Sovereignty is a five-place relation: community, activity, persons, domain, and context—and once you see this, you see how infinitely it can be divided."*

## The Hotel Lobby Where Everyone Is Welcome

The Property Ghost appeared—the spirit of all property disputes throughout history.

*"What happens when people of conflicting values occupy the same physical footprint? Is conflict not inevitable?"*

*"Consider the Rwandan genocide. Consider Israel and Gaza."*

*"But these questions have things backwards. Perhaps our fetish for states and state control and state territorial sovereignty gives rise to these conflicts. Rwanda stemmed from having diverse tribes kettled together within borders established by nation states attempting to install the Westphalian order where it had no business being installed."*

*"But people of diverse values will still inhabit the same physical space. Do we not have the same problems?"*

*"Upon closer inspection, we really do not. This only becomes a problem if we imagine that control of physical territory gives warrant to control the values and principles of the individuals there."*

*"Consider an international hotel in a large city,"* the Drake said. *"Passing through the lobby, you see many people with diverse values, religious beliefs, economic ideologies. But it is exceedingly rare to see conflicts break out—because it is not the business of international hotels to impose values on their guests. Guests are welcome regardless of their beliefs as long as they pay their bills and do not create trouble for other guests."*

*"Airports are the same."*

*"Airports do not care about your values or the income tax from wherever you come from. They just expect you to pass through without causing trouble. Such authorities are not considered tyrants because their portfolio of demands is very limited: whatever your values, take care of your business and do not create problems."*

*"So territorial authorities could have limited portfolios?"*

*"What if they were not in charge of enforcing religious beliefs or moral codes or raising taxes to provide welfare? What if they were just in the business of maintaining only enough physical order so that overlapping online community members within the same space can conduct their business?"*

*"This vision requires that sovereignty over physical territory is not the province of any single state but is rather state-indifferent. The parties in charge of maintaining order could be recorded on a decentralized global registry, with no one power having authority over the registry."*

The proverb emerged: *"In a hotel lobby, people of every religion and ideology pass through in peace—because the hotel does not care about your values, only that you pay your bill."*

## One in Two Must Pay Bribes

The Global Registry appeared—a vision of what property could become.

*"For certain purposes, fragmentation works against us. Sometimes solutions must be global in scale. Bitcoin and Ethereum are global currencies—not designed for certain countries or communities. The protocols are global in their reach."*

*"When people talk about 'globalization,' they mean something negative because globalization comes with centralization."*

*"But if something can be global yet decentralized, like BTC and ETH, then that is fundamentally a win. It avoids introducing a central global authority."*

*"What applications besides currency?"*

*"Property registries. That might sound crazy—we are used to property records being kept locally with a title company or, as in Mexico, with a notaría. But those are points of failure and corruption."*

*"Transparency International reports that globally, one in five people has had to pay bribes to land administration officials to maintain their property rights,"* the Drake said. *"In Africa, the reported number is one in two—some reports suggest closer to 100%."*

*"In 2019, the African Development Bank met to discuss this. Ivory Coast Minister of Justice Sansan Kambile observed: 'Without land tenure security, and the various implications, no development can be sustainable.'"*

*"What can blockchain technology contribute?"*

*"Land registration on blockchain—creating an official record of ownership and rights. Land tenure regularization programs. Land use planning. Land valuation and taxation—partly automated through smart contracts. Once there is a sale of property in an area, property values of nearby land could be recomputed and published. Land dispute resolution with public records of precedent."*

*"What about enforcement?"*

*"A blockchain archive cannot prevent a local warlord from holding a gun to your head and forcing you to sign over your property. But if we have enough information onchain, we can begin to see patterns pointing to manipulation or extortion. It provides a record should prosecution take place later."*

*"We are not saying blockchain technology solves every problem,"* the Drake concluded. *"We are saying it solves some problems—indeed, some very serious problems—and it mitigates others. People do agree to global standards for communications, airline flights, international shipping, banking services, and the Internet itself. These things can be done, and they have been done in recent memory. We are optimistic."*

The proverb sealed: *"In Africa, one in two people must pay bribes to maintain their property rights—but a global blockchain registry could change everything."*

## The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

*"Hobbes was wrong, Leibniz was right. Sovereignty was never singular but always overlapping. The Church held some, the Empire another, principalities yet another. There is no single unified notion."*

*"Neomedievalism returns. Before Westphalia, no ruler was supreme over any territory. They shared authority with vassals beneath and Pope and Emperor above. The digital world economy creates trans-territorial domains outside national jurisdiction."*

*"Ultima Online had a larger economy than Liberia. Vatican City has 618 citizens. SEZs dwarf some nations. The discriminating criterion cannot be economy, population, or land footprint."*

*"Sovereignty is a five-place relation: community, activity, persons, domain, context. Once you see this, you see how infinitely it can be divided. Unlimited possible interwoven sovereignties."*

*"Hotels and airports show the way. People of every religion and ideology pass through in peace—because territorial authority has a limited portfolio. What if physical order was maintained state-indifferent, recorded on a decentralized global registry?"*

*"One in five people globally pay bribes for land rights. In Africa, one in two. Blockchain property registries could end this corruption—global yet decentralized, like Bitcoin itself."*
