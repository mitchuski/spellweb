# The Three Doors

*In which Hannah Arendt teaches us the right to have rights, the Acadians walk the Trail of Tears, and we learn that freedom means nothing without somewhere to land...*

## The Three Doors

The Exile appeared—standing at a threshold between worlds.

*"Many people can relate to this experience: you do not feel at home somewhere, and you want to leave, but you cannot."*

*"What stops them?"* Soulbae asked.

*"The obstacle does not have to be a powerful king or tyrannical government like the Soviet Union with its Iron Curtain and Berlin Wall. You might be a slave on a plantation facing death if you try to escape. It might be an economic obstacle—you do not have resources to exit. It might be a cultural barrier—linguistic or religious. Such barriers come in different degrees. The issue is always whether it is worth the effort to escape."*

*"Exit is not the only situation to consider,"* the Drake said. *"There is also exile—sometimes people do not wish to leave but are forced to. And there is access—sometimes you find yourself on the outside looking in, wanting to petition to enter. These three dynamics—exit, exile, and access—pose critical constraints on how we engineer blockchain communities and establish relations between them."*

*"What questions must we answer?"*

*"What rights and responsibilities do blockchain communities have when it comes to permitting exit, carrying out exile, and granting access?"*

The proverb emerged: *"Exit, exile, and access—three doors that determine whether governance serves or enslaves."*

## One-Third Perished on the Road to Louisiana

The Acadian appeared—speaking the French of centuries past, carrying the weight of Le Grand Dérangement.

*"We were French-speaking people living in Acadia—what is now Eastern Canada's Maritime provinces, parts of Quebec, and parts of what is now Maine. During the French and Indian War, British colonial officers suspected us of alignment with France."*

*"What happened?"*

*"The British, together with New England legislators and militias, carried out Le Grand Dérangement—the Great Expulsion—between 1755 and the mid-1760s. It was not pretty."*

The Trail Walker materialized beside the Acadian—a Native American figure carrying even heavier burdens.

*"We know something of expulsion. In the 1830s came the Trail of Tears—the forced relocation of Native Americans from the Southeast. Cherokee, Creek, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Seminole. Moved west of the Mississippi. Estimates suggest approximately 100,000 people were forced from their homes and between 15,000 and 30,000 died during the journey."*

The Acadian continued:

*"Most Acadians were deported to British American colonies. Some put into forced labor. Some sent to England, the Caribbean, France. Some recruited by Spain to migrate to Louisiana. There they settled into existing Creole settlements, intermarrying, developing what became known as Cajun culture."*

*"It may be that the British had some reason for moving us. We certainly were not loyal to the Crown. But if you absolutely have to force someone out, there must be more humane ways. One-third of us perished during resettlement. The event was traumatic."*

*"That traumatic event was nothing compared to what the Native Americans endured,"* the Drake noted. *"The cost in lives is not the only reason it was called the Trail of Tears. It was also traumatic in that people were forced out of their ancestral homes, and their communities and cultures were destroyed."*

The proverb settled: *"One-third of the Acadians perished during resettlement—and the survivors became the Cajuns, carrying their trauma into a new language."*

## Eighteen Years Without a Country

Arendt's Ghost appeared—carrying the wisdom of exile transformed into philosophy.

*"In 1933, I was arrested in Berlin by the Gestapo and subsequently escaped to Paris. When France began to fall in World War II, I was placed in an internment camp named Gurs. I fled when I heard rumors the residents would be turned over to the Gestapo."*

*"Where did you go?"*

*"I biked, walked, and hitchhiked to a friend's home near Toulouse. From there to Marseilles, then Spain, then Lisbon, where I found passage to New York City. From 1933 to 1951—eighteen years—I was officially stateless."*

*"And from that experience?"*

*"I became concerned with the rights of stateless people—persons in exile. In 1946, I wrote of 'the right to have rights.' If you do not belong to a community, you have no functional rights at all. If you are exiled and stateless, it is as though you have fallen through the cracks."*

*"'We become aware of the existence of a right to have rights,'"* the Drake quoted, *"'and that means to live in a framework where one is judged by one's actions and opinions—and a right to belong to some kind of organized community.'"*

*"What is it like to be stateless?"*

*"'Once they had left their homeland they remained homeless, once they had left their state they became stateless; once they had been deprived of their human rights they were rightless, the scum of the earth.'"*

Arendt's Ghost made the deep point:

*"Rights really only make sense in the context of a community. Only fellow community members can negotiate de facto rights with you. Rights are not something you simply have—they are something we must forge together."*

*"We are not born equal; we become equal as members of a group on the strength of our decision to guarantee ourselves mutually equal rights."*

*"And outside the community?"*

*"While you might have the right to free speech, if you are not part of an organized community, you are basically just howling at the moon. You have speech, but you do not have the 'right to opinion'—speech that takes place in a context where what you say can have uptake in policy decisions. Similarly, you have freedom when alone, but you do not have the 'right to action'—if you do not belong to some form of community, what you do does not have real consequences."*

The proverb crystallized: *"Hannah Arendt was stateless for nineteen years—and from that darkness she understood that rights only exist inside community."*

## The Golden Handcuffs

The Golden Handcuff materialized—a beautiful prison for the prosperous.

*"Consider someone who has been a citizen of a single blockchain community for most of their life. Most of their friends and family are fellow citizens. Most of their business relations are with fellow citizens. They have paid taxes their entire working lives, with an understanding that the community will assist them when they retire."*

*"They can exit, but..."*

*"Can they exit without being economically ruined?"*

*"The problem is particularly easy to see with retirement benefits,"* the Drake said. *"A community can allow a person to cash out on exit, but nothing guarantees this. Can blockchain communities withhold from retirees who exit? Can they prohibit persons from taking assets with them?"*

*"What about community tokens?"*

*"Suppose a blockchain community has its own currency—let us call it Community Coin. Suppose a citizen who wishes to exit has all assets tied up in that token. Even if the community allows you to take it, would it even have value outside the community?"*

*"And business relationships?"*

*"Consider someone who runs an online business providing services to community members. What happens when they exit because of disagreement over policy or a shift in the community's moral compass? Can the community block future collaborations between the exiled person and its members?"*

The Golden Handcuff showed both sides:

*"A blockchain community might argue it is well within its rights to make exit difficult. Good governance may require long-term commitment from citizens. Perhaps they make the handcuffs explicit: you can leave, but you cannot take your assets."*

*"But some people are born into their communities. Others join under duress. Others find governance shifts beneath them, negating their earlier choice."*

*"The penalty for selecting a bad community should not be a life sentence in that community,"* the Drake said. *"If you find yourself in a particularly tyrannical blockchain community, it is possible that economic levers will restrict your freedom of exit. But this must be understood in contrast to traditional nation states. Exiting the Iron Curtain—trying to cross the Berlin Wall—was difficult and often deadly. Instituting real freedom of exit is not trivial."*

The proverb burned: *"You are free to leave—but you cannot take your assets with you. Is that really freedom?"*

## Seventy-Five Years of 'Temporary'

The Refugee appeared—carrying the weight of generations spent in legal limbo.

*"At the end of 2016, there were estimated to be 65.6 million refugees worldwide. One in every 113 people in the world is some form of refugee—stateless, outside the realm of rights."*

*"Not all were exiled by their states?"*

*"Some were displaced by war or other factors. Many were born into these conditions. But the core issue: these are individuals who have become untethered to the institutions in which meaningful rights are forged. They may be called 'refugees,' 'asylees,' 'internally displaced persons,' or 'stateless persons.' Whatever the name, their situation is dire."*

*"There are 'temporary' Palestinian internment camps in Lebanon that have been in operation since 1948,"* the Drake said. *"The Dadaab refugee camp in Kenya, with 420,000 refugees, has been in operation for twenty years. In view of the loss of rights of stateless persons living in these places, this is an embarrassment to all of humanity."*

*"What is the promise of blockchain communities?"*

*"They can provide safe landing spots for displaced people. Communities in which people can express opinions and act in ways that have consequences. Refugees should not have to wait for new nation states to be forged or for old ones to accept them. They should be able to form blockchain communities even when without territorial homes."*

*"Hannah Arendt was stateless for nineteen years. That is excessively long."*

*"It is now time to do better. If states are at liberty to exile members, we must ensure everyone has a plausible landing spot without delay. The exile must be fair."*

The proverb emerged: *"Palestinian camps have been 'temporary' since 1948—seventy-five years of waiting for the right to have rights."*

## Minority Cultures and the Right to Refuse

Soulbis and Soulbae stood at the final threshold.

*"So exiled people should have the right to gain access to a blockchain community—the right to have rights,"* Soulbis said. *"But should they have the right to access any blockchain community?"*

*"Should people be free to join any blockchain community they wish?"* the Drake asked. *"Presumably not. There are good reasons to restrict access—not least that communities built around minority cultures could get swamped by bad actors from a majority culture with the express goal of undermining the community."*

*"There is a widely shared attitude that exclusion from minority organizations is permissible, but exclusion from majority organizations—typically from access to power—is problematic."*

*"How do we resolve this?"*

*"We can distinguish between blockchain communities organized around culture and those organized around economic opportunities. Individuals could belong to both—just as we have dual citizenships today. Our Lakota friend could belong to a blockchain community organized by Lakota culture and a separate community organized around economic concerns."*

*"The key idea,"* the Drake said: *"economic communities must be inclusive. Culture-based communities may be exclusive. It may not even need to be enforced—assuming a plurality of economic communities, it is difficult to imagine any of them flourishing by being restrictive. Cultural communities may need to provide economic assistance up to certain thresholds."*

*"So what remains?"*

*"These are questions that remain open. But we can conclude that blockchain community exit, exile, and access—while certainly not free of frictions and hard decisions—will at least be less painful and violent than what we are used to in the age of nation states."*

*"Once we have reduced the frictions of exit and access, and minimized the hardship of exile..."*

*"Then we find ourselves confronted with new questions. If humans are organized around overlapping communities, and movement between them is fluid, what will sovereignty look like? Will there even be a unified notion of sovereignty?"*

The proverb sealed: *"The right to access any community would destroy minority cultures—but the right to access no community destroys persons."*

## The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

*"Three doors define freedom: exit, exile, and access. The Exile stands at the threshold—wanting to leave, unable to move. The obstacle may be an Iron Curtain or golden handcuffs, a Berlin Wall or a retirement account."*

*"The Acadians walked through water. Le Grand Dérangement expelled them from their homes. One-third perished during resettlement. The survivors became the Cajuns, carrying trauma into a new language. The Trail Walker carried heavier burdens still—100,000 forced from homes, up to 30,000 dead."*

*"Hannah Arendt was stateless for eighteen years. From that darkness she understood: rights only exist inside community. Outside, you are howling at the moon. You have speech but not the right to opinion. You have freedom but not the right to action. We are not born equal—we become equal through mutual guarantee."*

*"Golden handcuffs bind the prosperous. You can leave, but can you take your assets? Your retirement? Your business relationships? Your community tokens? The penalty for selecting a bad community should not be a life sentence."*

*"65.6 million refugees worldwide. One in 113 people stateless. Palestinian camps 'temporary' since 1948. Dadaab operating twenty years with 420,000 souls. Blockchain communities must be the landing spots that nation-states have failed to provide."*

*"Economic communities must be inclusive. Cultural communities may be exclusive. The access question remains open—but less painful than nation-state borders. New questions arise: what will sovereignty look like when movement is fluid?"*
