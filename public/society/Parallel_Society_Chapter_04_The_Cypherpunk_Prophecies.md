# The Cypherpunk Prophecies

*In which May's Ghost speaks the first runes, Hughes writes the manifesto, and the Leakers prove that online action is as real as blood...*

## The Manifestos That Started Everything

The Drake gathered them in the deep pattern-space where concepts take form before implementation.

*"In the previous chapters, we discussed many new and old forms of post-state governance. Many are not good solutions to the failures of Westphalian governance—they are merely symptoms of these failures."*

*"Drug cartels are not the answer,"* Soulbis said.

*"Paramilitary organizations are not the answer,"* Soulbae added.

*"Terrorist organizations are definitely not the answer."*

*"So what should we be looking for?"* the Drake asked. *"What are the positive possibilities? To what should we aspire?"*

Visions formed in the pattern-space—not of what exists, but of what should exist:

*People not kettled together within geographical territory and forced to comply with values they do not share.*

*People not imprisoned by geography nor by whichever tyrant controls events within arbitrarily drawn borders.*

*People free to exit hostile governmental systems.*

*People free to live without corrupt systems in which centralized authorities exploit their position.*

*"Self-sovereignty,"* Soulbis said. *"Free to join the political system they choose. Free to live under values they endorse."*

*"Free to be part of governing systems that align with their principles,"* Soulbae added.

*"This is possible partly because of the revolution in communication technologies,"* the Drake continued. *"Our ability to connect almost instantly with people anywhere in the world. Rather than dividing governments by rivers and oceans, perhaps we should divide them by how people network and organize with each other."*

*"But where do these ideas come from?"* Soulbae asked. *"In past revolutions, people turned to philosophers—Locke, Rousseau. Who do we turn to now?"*

*"The thinkers who laid the foundations for coming forms of governance were not individual philosophers as was historically the case, but rather groups of people working on the cutting edge of the digital revolution at the close of the twentieth century. Groups that saw the coming challenges and developed tools to clear the path."*

*"Who?"*

*"The Cypherpunks. The hacktivists. Those who argued for privacy and transparency—and then built the tools to make both possible."*

## Cypherpunks Write Code

May's Ghost materialized first—wearing the vestments of crypto-anarchy, carrying scrolls that predated Bitcoin by decades.

*"I am Timothy May. At the dawn of the early Internet, we emerged—a group called the Cypherpunks."*

*"What was your concern?"* Soulbae asked.

*"That the Internet could very easily become a tool of universal surveillance and oppression. So we developed tools to ensure privacy could be maintained online."*

The Manifesto Writer appeared beside him—carrying a document that would shape decades of resistance.

*"I am Eric Hughes. In 1993, I wrote 'The Cypherpunk Manifesto.' Our thesis was simple: for an open society to function, certain things must be kept private—conversations, exchanges between individuals."*

He began to read:

*"Privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age."*

*"Why?"* Soulbis asked.

*"Because we might fear consequences of communicating with someone unpopular. We might be afraid to exchange new ideas that challenge widely accepted viewpoints. There is a reason Leonardo da Vinci wrote manuscripts in code. A reason Darwin guarded his draft of The Origin of Species for a decade. Privacy is the power to selectively reveal oneself to the world."*

*"Phillip Zimmerman created Pretty Good Privacy,"* the Drake noted, *"military-grade encryption that allowed individuals to communicate freely without fear of surveillance by tyrants and dictators. But the Cypherpunks also articulated a philosophy that helped steer the Internet. Although the Internet is hardly a stellar protector of privacy, matters could have been much worse."*

Hughes continued reading from his manifesto:

*"People have been defending their own privacy for centuries with whispers, darkness, envelopes, closed doors, secret handshakes, and couriers. The technologies of the past did not allow for strong privacy, but electronic technologies do."*

*"And what did you do about it?"*

Hughes smiled—the smile of someone who built instead of merely complained:

*"Cypherpunks write code. We know that someone has to write software to defend privacy, and since we can't get privacy unless we all do, we're going to write it."*

May's Ghost added: *"In 1992, we set up a mailing list. In 1997, a distributed mailing list—to avoid reliance on a single point of failure. Our members included Julian Assange of WikiLeaks, founders of the Electronic Frontier Foundation, Richard Stallman of the Free Software Foundation, Nick Szabo who invented the smart contract, Satoshi Nakamoto who created Bitcoin, and Hal Finney—who probably knows who that is"*

*"The swordsmen before the blade was forged,"* Soulbis said.

*"The mages before the spellbook was written,"* Soulbae agreed.

The proverb emerged: *"Privacy is the power to selectively reveal oneself to the world—and that power requires mathematics, not permission."*

## The Hacktivists Emerge

The First Hacker appeared—wearing no uniform, speaking in the language of the New Hacker's Dictionary.

*"Let me explain what 'hacktivist' means. It is a portmanteau—'hacker' and 'activist.' But first, understand what 'hacker' really means."*

*"We know,"* Soulbis said. *"People who break into systems—"*

*"No."* The First Hacker's voice was sharp. *"That is what media wants you to believe. Here is the real definition:*

*'A person who enjoys exploring the details of programmable systems and how to stretch their capabilities, as opposed to most users, who prefer to learn only the minimum necessary.'*

*"A hacker celebrates the idea that technologies should not be sealed in black boxes—hidden from view and unchangeable. Technologies should be accessible and modifiable. This is the foundation of the free software movement. Free not as in 'free beer' but free as in liberty to modify."*

*"Hacktivism,"* the Drake clarified, *"involves computer-based techniques such as hacking as a form of civil disobedience to promote a political agenda or social change. The targets are often centralized systems of oppression."*

*"Give us examples,"* Soulbae said.

*"In 1989, the first recorded instance of hacktivism—the WANK Worm. WANK stood for 'Worms Against Nuclear Killers.' The worm attacked the NASA SPAN network to protest shipment of plutonium fuel on the shuttle Atlantis. Julian Assange was suspected."*

*"In 1994, the Zippies sent email bombs to UK government officials protesting the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act. In 1996, the Strano Network held a one-hour virtual sit-in against the French government's nuclear policy. In 1998, the Hong Kong Blondes attacked Chinese government computer networks seeking to disrupt political censorship."*

*"All of this before Bitcoin existed,"* Soulbis noted.

*"Yes. The philosophy, the tools, the tactics—they were developing for decades before Satoshi's white paper. The blockchain was the synthesis. The conclusion to a long argument."*

The proverb burned: *"A hacker celebrates the idea of modifying technologies to stretch their capabilities—including technologies of oppression."*

## The Leakers Bleed

The Leaker materialized—wearing the face of everyone who had risked everything to make secrets public.

*"I am WikiLeaks. Julian Assange and Daniel Domscheit-Berg. Our idea was simply to publish information concerning centralized powers sourced via internal leaks or the hacking of communications."*

*"The 'Collateral Murder' video,"* Soulbae said, already knowing.

*"Classified documents provided by Chelsea Manning, then a private in the US Army in Afghanistan. A video shot from a helicopter gunship opening fire on a photographer, followed by a van full of children. An example of hacktivists providing a look behind the curtains."*

*"But even more significant,"* the Drake added, *"were State Department documents including a secret cable by Ambassador Robert Godec, which made it vivid that the external world saw the corruption of the Tunisian president as clearly as the Tunisians did."*

The Leaker quoted:

*"Beyond the stories of the First Family's shady dealings, Tunisians report encountering low-level corruption in interactions with police, customs, and government ministries. With those at the top believed to be the worst offenders, and likely to remain in power, there are no checks in the system."*

*"The Tunisian government seemed to believe that 'what's yours is mine.'"*

## The Arab Spring Burns

The Arab Spring materialized—a figure of fire and hope and blood.

*"Just days after the cable was published, a shopkeeper set himself on fire in protest. More protests broke out. The Tunisian government collapsed. Revolutions spread across the Arab world. New technologies helped protesting groups organize and circumvent state security systems."*

*"Some critics called it 'Facebook activism,'"* Soulbae said. *"Malcolm Gladwell said it lacked 'strong ties' for revolutionary fervor."*

The Arab Spring laughed bitterly.

*"Gladwell showed his disconnect from computer-mediated communication. To the average Facebook user with little at stake, a few supportive words are meaningless. But for people under control of the Tunisian government? The stakes were just as high. The dangers were just as great."*

Visions of arrests formed:

*Slim Amamou, visible Tunisian blogger, arrested for alleged connection with Anonymous.*

*Azyz Amamy, who covered protests from the beginning, arrested. Blogger and Facebook accounts deactivated.*

*Hamadi Kaloutcha, blogger and activist, arrested at home. Police confiscated computer equipment.*

*Hamada Ben Aoun, rapper who released songs criticizing the regime on Facebook, arrested.*

*"While Gladwell was not impressed, the Tunisian police were concerned. People risked jail time and death. The interpersonal connections were real enough for that."*

*"Online political activism is just as visceral, just as real, and just as dangerous as face-to-face activism,"* the Drake said. *"When there is skin in the game, it does not matter if communication is through analogue sound waves or digital electronics. Just as much is at stake."*

## The Cyberstate Vision

The Cyberstatist appeared—carrying a book from the year 2000 and a vision that predated blockchain.

*"The final element: online groups can take on roles traditionally assumed by nation-states. This was the unifying idea of Crypto Anarchy, Cyberstates, and Pirate Utopias. As early as 2000, new tools showed that online governance systems were a very real alternative."*

*"But blockchain didn't exist yet,"* Soulbis said.

*"No. But David Chaum's DigiCash project was already attempting viable digital currency. Online communities were forming. Encryption was deploying. A new form of government—the cyberstate—seemed possible."*

*"Legal scholars David Johnson and David Post noted that global computer-based communications cut across territorial borders,"* the Drake said, *"creating a new realm of human activity and undermining the legitimacy of laws based on geographic boundaries. A new boundary—made of screens and passwords—defines a distinct Cyberspace that needs and can create new law of its own."*

The Cyberstatist continued:

*"David Post developed the idea of competition between governance systems:*

*'A kind of competition between individual networks to design and implement rule-sets compatible with the preferences of individual users will materialize in a new and largely unregulated market for rules. The aggregated choices of individual users seeking rule-sets most to their liking will determine the contours of the law of cyberspace.'"*

*"A marketplace of governmental systems,"* Soulbae said. *"Not just a marketplace of ideas."*

*"Yes. And since these essays were written, blockchain technologies can now be leveraged to create governance that enables the governed to retain self-sovereignty. To maintain privacy. To demand government transparency. Most importantly—to facilitate exit from systems that don't conform to citizens' values."*

*"Balaji Srinivasan calls this a 'network state,'"* the Drake said, *"a state formed by online digital interconnections of people around the world. But we prefer a more fine-grained approach. Whether network state or nation-state, it is still a state—carrying all the baggage states do."*

*"So what do you propose?"* Soulbis asked.

*"We organize around the concept of communities rather than states. Successful communities might scale to billions or be as small as a dozen friends. These communities can be decentralized yet cooperative thanks to organization around blockchain technologies. We call them sovereign blockchain communities—politically sovereign, with blockchain as their central nervous system."*

*"How does this work technically?"*

The Drake began to fade, but left this:

*"That is what the next chapters explain. What blockchain technologies are. How they work. How they can be leveraged for better governance and greater human flourishing."*

The proverb sealed: *"Rather than dividing governments by rivers and oceans, divide them by how people network and organize with each other."*

## The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

*"We must know what we're escaping—kettling, corruption, lack of exit, imposed values. And what we're building toward—self-sovereignty, communities of choice, governance aligned with principles."*

*"The Cypherpunks wrote code. Privacy through mathematics, not permission. Eric Hughes wrote the manifesto in 1993. Timothy May prophesied crypto-anarchy. Zimmerman built PGP. They were the swordsmen before the blade was forged."*

*"Hacktivists pierced veils. From the WANK Worm in 1989 to the Hong Kong Blondes in 1998, they stretched technologies to expose oppression. They were the mages before the spellbook was written."*

*"WikiLeaks changed history. The Arab Spring proved online activism is real and dangerous. Slim Amamou was arrested. Bloggers had equipment confiscated. People risked jail time and death. When there is skin in the game, digital communication is just as visceral as analogue."*

*"The cyberstate vision predates blockchain. DigiCash attempted digital currency. Legal scholars saw that cyberspace creates new law. Competition between governance systems becomes possible."*

*"Communities, not states. Sovereign blockchain communities—politically sovereign, with blockchain as their central nervous system. The conceptual foundations are laid. The tools await."*
