# When Rights Became Real

*In which the Declaration of 1789 meets blockchain, we learn that de jure rights require de facto systems, and communities discover they have duties to each other...*

---

## Lafayette's Gift to the World

Lafayette's Ghost appeared—the Marquis who had fought in both revolutions, carrying a document that shaped the world.

*"In 1789, during the French Revolution, the National Assembly of France approved a document known as the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen."*

*"Who wrote it?"*

*"Initially drafted by me—George Washington's protégé, the Marquis de Lafayette—with assistance from Thomas Jefferson, then serving as US Ambassador to France. The final version was written by the Abbé Sieyès. It incorporated Enlightenment philosophy from John Locke and Jean-Jacques Rousseau, as well as inspiration from the US Declaration of Independence."*

The Declaration materialized—the document itself speaking:

*"To this day, I am a model for understanding human rights and am considered a valid document to which to appeal in constitutional and international law."*

*"But where do these rights come from? Who makes sure we are free to exercise them?"*

*"The rights seem to apply to individuals,"* the Drake said, *"but could we envision a similar document that applied to communities rather than individuals? What would it look like? And would we have the technology necessary to protect these rights?"*

The proverb emerged: *"Lafayette drafted the Declaration with Jefferson's help—but where do these rights actually come from?"*

## The Gap Between Paper and Power

The De Facto appeared—a figure representing the gap between rights on paper and rights in practice.

*"Let us distinguish between the de jure rights that someone might have—for example, rights that God might grant to them—and the de facto rights they are actually able to enjoy."*

*"Give us an example."*

*"If God were the foundation for our rights, God might grant us the right to liberty. But state authorities might take that right away in practice. In this case, one has a God-given, de jure right to liberty but no corresponding de facto right, as the state has stripped it from us."*

*"There are probably as many theories of the origins of de jure rights as there are thinkers on the topic,"* the Drake said:

- *"Saint Aquinas—All rights are conferred by God"*
- *"John Locke—Natural rights to life, liberty, and property are inherent and inalienable"*
- *"Jean-Jacques Rousseau—De jure rights grounded in the social contract and 'general will'"*
- *"Jeremy Bentham—Rights extrude from maximizing happiness for the greatest number"*

*"Which theory is correct?"*

*"We remain neutral on the source of de jure rights. For current purposes, it does not matter where they come from. We just need to agree on what some of those rights are—for example, the right to liberty or political self-determination."*

*"Then what matters?"*

*"Once we agree on what those individual rights might be, we immediately get to the question of whether we will be allowed to exercise them. Do we have the corresponding de facto rights? What sorts of systems are actually capable of protecting our individual rights?"*

*"Nation states?"*

*"We think nation states are, by their very nature, not capable of protecting the de jure rights of individuals. They can hardly be expected to do so when individuals with conflicting values are kettled within their borders. Any centralised authority faces the dangers of corruption and lack of transparency."*

The De Facto's key insight:

*"If governing institutions are responsible for protecting our de facto rights, they must be given the resources to do so. 'Ought' implies 'can.' Therefore, if they are to fulfill that responsibility, they must possess the means—and thus the rights—necessary to do so. This is how community rights emerge from individual rights."*

The proverb crystallized: *"God might grant you the right to liberty—but if the state strips it away, you have only a de jure right with no de facto reality."*

## The Seventeen Articles Speak

The Declaration spoke its seventeen articles, and the Drake analyzed:

*"Not all of these articles apply to individuals. Article XV, for example, speaks of the right that a society has to the accountability of its administrators—an example of a group right."*

*"ARTICLE XV. Every community has a right to demand of all its agents an account of their conduct."*

*"Article XVI is not really a right but rather a responsibility—and it is a responsibility of the society rather than individuals:"*

*"ARTICLE XVI. Every community in which a separation of powers and a security of rights is not provided for, wants a constitution."*

*"This suggests that if a society does not guarantee the rights of individual citizens, it is a false society—or in any case, it is without a true constitution."*

The Community Voice materialized:

*"Our focus here is more on communities than individuals. We ultimately propose a Declaration of the Rights of Communities—understanding communities broadly to include blockchain communities and any similarly organised group of individuals (decentralized yet cooperative)."*

*"But to get at the rights of communities, we first need to discuss what their responsibilities are, then reflect on their de jure rights, and from there think about whether the underlying technology stack can underwrite the corresponding de facto rights."*

The proverb settled: *"Not all of the Declaration's articles apply to individuals—some speak of the rights and responsibilities of communities themselves."*

## What Communities Owe Their Members

The Community Voice continued:

*"The responsibilities of blockchain communities are of two kinds: external responsibilities and internal responsibilities."*

*"Tell us the internal responsibilities first."*

*"We have argued that it is critical that citizens have the right to exit. If the right to exit is to be more than an empty platitude, we need to give it teeth."*

The Drake formalized:

*"I. RESPONSIBILITIES TO INDIVIDUALS*

*Communities have the primary responsibility to ensure individual exit. This responsibility entails additional responsibilities:*

*1. Communities have the responsibility to be as transparent as possible about their actions, plans, and goals. Reliable records should be easily accessible to all community members.*

*2. Communities have the responsibility to enable members the right to access information outside of the community.*

*3. Communities have the responsibility to ensure that community members do not forfeit their ability to exit even if they wish to do so.*

*4. Communities have the responsibility to ensure that their members retain access to the resources necessary to exit, should they choose to do so."*

*"And external responsibilities?"*

*"II. RESPONSIBILITIES TO OTHER COMMUNITIES*

*1. Communities have the responsibility to not interfere with the conduct of any community that is operating in a way that respects the rights of other communities and the ability of their members to exit.*

*2. Communities have the responsibility to be accurate and transparent with communities with which they wish to collaborate."*

*"What about proselytization?"*

*"A community has a right to control the information that enters it, but not over the right for members to venture out and explore the information space. Insofar as a community allows you into their information space, you play by its rules. But if someone ventures out, there really are no rules—proselytize away."*

*"And cultures that require ignorance to survive?"*

*"Any culture that requires involuntary censorship and forced ignorance to ensure its survival is perhaps not a culture worth preserving."*

The proverb burned: *"The right to exit means nothing without transparency—you cannot leave if you do not know what your leaders are doing."*

## Do Not Run to the Parents

The Decentralized Judge appeared—a figure that embodied judgment without centralization.

*"What happens if blockchain communities shirk their responsibilities or act contrary to them? What if they interfere with another community, or create barriers to exit for citizens?"*

*"Who determines when an agent or community has violated rights? What is to be done when violations occur?"*

*"For obvious reasons,"* the Drake said, *"we do not advocate a centralized authority to judge when communities are acting in violation of norms. We do not advocate a standing (or even temporary) centralized authority to act against such violations. The guiding philosophy of this project is that centralized authority is an inadequate solution to problems of governance in today's world."*

*"Then how?"*

*"In a world of decentralised governance, the preferred way to resolve disputes is not to 'run to the parents.' Disputes and misunderstandings often can be resolved on a peer-to-peer basis without appeal to higher authority."*

*"What if peer resolution fails?"*

*"Depending upon the severity of a violation and assuming the exhaustion of negotiated solutions, communities may organize themselves into temporary alliances to take action against communities that are violating norms. Following the principle that governance should be decentralized, these alliances should be fleeting—in place just long enough to respond to imminent threats and no longer."*

*"What can such an alliance do?"*

*"Success for a blockchain community is a direct function of its ability to network successfully with other communities. Being a pariah endangers this successful networking and thus endangers the ability of such a community to prosper."*

*"So the sanction is disconnection?"*

*"In the context of terrestrial nation states, sanctions are a crude instrument because traditional states have direct control over physical territory and resources. But blockchain communities do not project force the same way. Their most powerful mode of action is to disconnect and isolate bad actors."*

*"It is easier to disconnect someone from a communication network than to remove them from physical territory."*

The proverb emerged: *"When norms are violated, do not run to the parents—disputes should be resolved peer-to-peer, and enforcement through network disconnection."*

## The Declaration of the Rights of Communities

Soulbis and Soulbae stood before the emerging Declaration of the Rights of Communities.

*"We have seen that blockchain communities have important responsibilities—both to their members and to other communities,"* Soulbis said.

*"And from these responsibilities emerge rights,"* Soulbae added.

*"If individuals have a right to exit, then communities have the responsibility to make that a de facto right,"* the Drake said. *"But then communities, in turn, must have rights of their own. And if those community rights are also to be de facto rights, then we need to ensure that the underlying technology enables those rights."*

*"What are the community rights that emerge?"*

*"Communities have the right to:*
- *Exist unmolested as long as they do not harm other communities*
- *Control the information that enters their network space*
- *Set conditions for membership and exit*
- *Form alliances with other communities*
- *Disconnect from communities that violate norms*
- *Maintain transparent records accessible to members*
- *Develop their own cultures and values"*

*"And the key insight?"*

*"There are clear norms that govern the conduct of all blockchain communities—surrounding the right to exit, for example. But judging that something is a violation of these norms and taking action in response is not the province of centralized authority."*

*"Violations of norms can only be recognized by individuals and individual communities. Their principal mode of action is network effects—the power to include or exclude."*

*"Blockchain communities will have significant rights of their own,"* the Drake concluded. *"The question is how we can ensure that those rights become de facto rights, not merely aspirational ideas. Our aim is to offer a template for discussing these de jure community rights, with an eye to organizing the technology stack to afford them these abilities—in other words, to make them de facto rights."*

The proverb sealed: *"Rights without technology are wishes—the blockchain is how we make community rights into de facto realities."*

## The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

*"The Declaration of 1789 was drafted by Lafayette with Jefferson's help, finalized by Sieyès. A model for individual rights. But where do these rights come from? God? Nature? Social contract? Utility?"*

*"De jure vs de facto. God might grant you the right to liberty—but if the state strips it away, you have only a right on paper. Nation states are not capable of protecting individual rights when conflicting values are kettled within borders."*

*"'Ought' implies 'can.' If communities are responsible for protecting rights, they must have the means to do so. Community rights emerge from individual rights."*

*"Responsibilities to individuals: ensure exit, be transparent, enable outside information access, prevent forfeiture of exit ability, ensure resources for departure. Responsibilities to communities: do not interfere, be accurate and transparent in collaboration."*

*"Decentralized oversight is possible. Peer-to-peer resolution first. Temporary alliances form to address violations, then dissolve. The sanction is disconnection—isolation from network effects. It is easier to disconnect than to remove from physical territory."*

*"Rights without technology are wishes. Blockchain is how we make community rights into de facto realities."*

The master pattern emerged:

*Declaration → Jure → Facto → Community → Responsibilities → Decentralized*
